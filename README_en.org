* Background
- Location: Kilimanjaro Region, Marangu
- Climate & Environment: 1,300 m a.s.l., moderate climate, temperatures < 30°C. High dust exposure (fine, red sand).
- Vocational school with 120 students, 60 per grade
- Trades taught:
  - CJ, Carpentry and Joinery
  - EL, Electrical Installation
  - MB, Masonry and Bricklaying
  - MVM, Motor Vehicle Mechanics
  - DSCT, Design Sewing and Cloth Technology
  - ICT, Information and Communications Technology
- Computer equipment: 20 Business PCs (HP ProDesk/EliteDesk SFF) from approx. 2010-2015. 2 GB RAM, not networked, but equipped with network cards. Mechanical hard drives with 240 GB.
- Monitors: approx. 15", equipped with HDMI inputs, but currently connected via analog VGA cables.
- Operating system(s): Windows 7 and Windows 10. Systems are likely heavily infected with viruses. "Communication" occurs via USB flash drives.
- Application software: Microsoft Office (version unknown), CAD (exact specification missing).
- Usage: Training of ICT students, CAD application for other trades.
- Internet access via LTE/5G router available. Can potentially be upgraded if necessary, e.g., Starlink.

** Problems
- Windows is no longer supported (apart from the fact that they are entirely pirated copies, which is common in Tanzania). An update to current Windows versions is impossible due to high hardware requirements and missing licenses.
- Physical limitations: High wear and tear on interfaces. Worn out or destroyed USB ports after an estimated 200 plug attempts due to brute force and dust.
- Ergonomic problems: Analog VGA signals lead to flickering and blurring in this environment, causing rapid eye fatigue when reading code.
- Replacing the computers is far too expensive.
- Practical education in networking and communication cannot take place at all.
- The students have no connection to the world's knowledge (internet access) within the school environment.
- Tight financial budget.

** Solution
- Adaptation of established solutions (LTSP).
- Building the new infrastructure as a school project.
- A server adapted to local conditions:
  - Cost-efficient
  - Durable (planned operational lifespan of 10 years)
  - License-free (Open Source Software)
  - Sufficient performance for 20 concurrent students
  - Remotely maintainable
  - Absolute priority on a near zero-maintenance setup. Forgoing expensive "bleeding-edge" tech.
- "Hardware Hardening": Consistent physical protection of the infrastructure (e.g., blocking unused ports to prevent dust ingress and user error, adding strain reliefs to cables).
- Quality improvement at the workstation: Transition from analog VGA to purely digital video transmission.
- The existing, reliable old business PCs of the students are perfectly suited to access the server. After commissioning, the student PCs will no longer use the old mechanical hard drives but will boot over the network as thin clients.
- Every student can receive their individual home directory. This should definitely be implemented for the ICT students; for the other students, it is an option.
- Automated and fault-tolerant student management following the "60 in, 60 out" principle.

** Component Procurement
- Strategic purchasing: Use of refurbished enterprise hardware (with warranty) for critical server components to achieve the highest quality within the budget.
- Bulk procurement: Consumables like mice, keyboards, or cables are bought in larger bulk packages to build high on-site redundancy (spare parts depot) at extremely low unit prices.
- Procurement of bulky items (case, UPS, possibly PSU) locally, e.g., through a local computer shop in Moshi (InnoSykes Computer).

** Assembly
- Server: Assembly by the local computer shop.
- Preparing the thin clients: Physical modifications (removal of HDDs, blocking of interfaces) on-site in the classroom.

** Transport to Tanzania
- Transport of small individual components in carry-on or checked luggage during the next visit from or to Tanzania.
- Discarding bulky retail packaging (e.g., OEM/business keyboards in flat brown boxes) to minimize packing volume for air travel.

** Initial Setup in Germany
- Installation and configuration of the server software.
- Preparation of automation scripts (e.g., batch import of students via a universal CSV format).
- Setup of the network, the router, and the VPN access.
- Disassembly of the server into its components.
- Distribution of the components for transport (in case customs in Tanzania raise any issues).

** On-Site Setup in Tanzania
- Hardware setup: Implementation of physical protection in the classroom (strain reliefs using zip ties on monitors, sacrificial cable strategy for USB ports on the server).
- Briefing: Short training for the local teachers on simple maintenance routines (e.g., monthly brushing of dust filters, swapping sacrificial cables).
- Software setup: Should be a push-button affair due to the professional assembly and preparation in Germany (though it surely won't be quite that easy).

* Network
- The school recently acquired well-functioning internet access via a cellular network (Airtel router).
- The student network gains internet access (or access to the Airtel router) via a MikroTik hAP ax3 router/firewall.
- The hAP ax3 router has built-in Wi-Fi to provide free Wi-Fi to the students. Bandwidth is throttled so that video streaming is impossible (at least not if multiple students try it simultaneously). Wi-Fi clients are isolated from each other and cannot access the student LAN. Access to the teacher network is blocked for both Wi-Fi and student LAN by the router's firewall.
- The connection to Germany is established via a VPN network (WireGuard).
- Since the new server has three network interfaces, a virtual machine can be set up as a backup router (optional) in case the hAP ax3 fails.
- Alternatively, an inexpensive MikroTik RouterBOARD could act as a backup (e.g., MikroTik RouterBOARD hEX S RB760iGS, approx. 70 €).
- A MikroTik CRS326-24G-2S+RM is used as the switch. This features 2 SFP+ ports for the fast server uplink and 24 1 GBit/s ports for the student PCs.

** Digression: Why not a used industrial server (rack server)?

During project planning, the question often arises why retired enterprise servers (e.g., Dell PowerEdge or HP ProLiant) are not purchased, as they are extremely cheap on the refurbished market. The deployment of such systems is prohibited for the following technical and infrastructural reasons:

*** 1. Noise Emission (Classroom Ergonomics)
Industrial rack servers (1U or 2U form factor) cool their densely packed components with small, high-speed fans that reach speeds of over 10,000 RPM. The resulting noise pollution completely precludes regular teaching in the same room. Since the school does not have a dedicated, air-conditioned, and soundproofed server room, the system must operate whisper-quiet (like our Noctua concept) inside the classroom.

*** 2. Lack of Dust Protection
Enterprise hardware is designed exclusively for operation in highly clean, air-conditioned data centers and features absolutely no dust filters by design. In the heavily particle-laden environment at Mount Kilimanjaro, the narrow cooling fins of these servers would clog with the red fine dust within a very short time. This inevitably leads to thermal failure. Our planned PC case solution with filtered positive air pressure is the only viable alternative here.

*** 3. Energy Inefficiency and Power Grid Load
Used rack servers whose purchase price would fit the project budget (e.g., older Intel Xeon architectures) exhibit an unacceptably high power consumption. A high idle draw as well as massive load spikes would instantly overload the planned UPS (750VA) and put additional strain on the already unstable local power grid. Throttling our modern AMD processor to a 65W TDP offers vastly superior efficiency per watt.

*** 4. Proprietary Spare Parts and Transport Logistics
Industrial servers use proprietary form factors for power supplies, motherboards, and fans. In the event of a hardware defect in Tanzania, sourcing matching spare parts is nearly impossible. Our custom server, on the other hand, is entirely based on the global ATX standard. In an absolute emergency, the local computer shop in Moshi can adapt components from standard desktop PCs as spare parts. Furthermore, a 19-inch server is simply too large and heavy to be transported cost-effectively and safely through customs in checked luggage to Tanzania.

- *Links:* [[https://www.google.com/search?q=Brennenstuhl+Premium-Protect-Line][Google]] | [[https://www.ebay.de/sch/i.html?_nkw=Brennenstuhl+Premium-Protect-Line&LH_SellerType=2][eBay Pro]]

*** Why this part?
Although the downstream UPS has internal protection mechanisms, a dedicated surge protector at the primary wall outlet is mandatory. It acts as an upstream "electrical sacrificial anode". In the event of catastrophic voltage spikes (e.g., from lightning strikes or severe malfunctions in the local power grid), this component absorbs the destructive force and physically breaks the circuit before the 400 Euro UPS or the server are damaged. If this protection mechanism breaks, only an inexpensive power strip needs to be replaced.

*** Advantages
- Most cost-efficient protection ("First Line of Defense") for the expensive core infrastructure.
- Significant reduction in the risk of total UPS failure during extreme grid anomalies.
- Visual/acoustic signaling upon loss of protection function (depending on the model).

*** Disadvantages
- After triggering during a massive strike, the strip is usually irreparably destroyed and must be replaced (keeping a spare device is highly recommended).

*** Alternatives
- Hardwired surge protection in the building's fuse box (Type 2 / Type 3 arrester). This offers even better protection but requires a local electrician and structural work on the school, which is outside our direct project scope.

** RAM: UDIMM 32GB DDR4-3200 ECC unbuffered
- *Unit Price:* approx. 190.00 € (Daily price)
- *Quantity:* 2 (Initial setup: 64 GB)
- *Links:* [[https://www.google.com/search?q=UDIMM+32GB+DDR4-3200+ECC+unbuffered][Google]] | [[https://www.ebay.de/sch/i.html?_nkw=UDIMM+32GB+DDR4-3200+ECC+unbuffered&LH_SellerType=2][eBay Pro]]

*** Why this part?
ECC (Error Correction Code) RAM is a mandatory technical requirement for the stable operation of the ZFS file system to proactively prevent data corruption from memory errors. Procurement is manufacturer-agnostic (e.g., Samsung, Hynix, Micron, Kingston) according to JEDEC specifications.

Due to the severe limitations of the student hardware (only 2 GB of local RAM), the architecture is primarily designed for a "thin client" mode. Here, the server handles all computational loads. To map this performantly, a two-stage, future-proof expansion plan is targeted:

- *Phase 1 (Initial setup 64 GB):* The system starts with 2x 32 GB modules. This prioritizes system stability and performance, as 64 GB provides sufficient resources to host 20 thin-client sessions (including desktop environment and basic applications) on the server side.
- *Phase 2 (Maximum expansion 128 GB):* Should requirements increase due to memory-intensive applications (like CAD) or heavier usage, two additional 32 GB modules can be retrofitted. By choosing 32 GB modules in Phase 1, the necessary RAM slots on the motherboard remain available, eliminating the need to discard existing hardware.

*** Advantages
- Hardware-level error correction (Mandatory for ZFS).
- Immediate readiness for thin-client operation.
- Future-proof upgrade path (no module displacement during later upgrades).

*** Disadvantages
- Significantly higher initial investment costs, as 32 GB UDIMM modules are relatively expensive on the market.

*** Alternatives
- Starting with 4x 16 GB (More cost-effective, but blocks all RAM slots and prevents future expansion to 128 GB).

The use of enterprise SSDs with Power-Loss-Protection (PLP) is essential to prevent data corruption during the frequent local power outages. Sourcing refurbished hardware offers enterprise specifications within the given budget framework.

6 of these SSDs are combined into a raidz2 array. This provides the system with a net capacity of 4x the individual capacity; meaning with 480 GB, we yield 1,920 GB, or nearly 2 TB. The RAID array is protected against the simultaneous failure of 2 SSDs. One SSD is kept in the cabinet as a reserve.

Procurement is done via the second-hand market on eBay or through refurbished hardware dealers.

*** Advantages
